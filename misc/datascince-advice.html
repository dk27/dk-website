Rambling on How to Become a Data Scientist. My 5 Cents

December 27th, 2020

You decided to become a Data Scientist. Now what?

Over the last few weeks, I saw posts on LinkedIn and Twitter from aspiring data scientists trying to get their feet wet in the industry which prompted me to writing this post.

I don't claim to know it all and my advice may not be applicable to all industries, but from my experience and research of other industries, I'm pretty sure at least some of it can be useful.

If you are an aspiring data scientist and reading this post, continue reading. If you are an experienced data scientist and reading this post and there is something that I forgot to mention, feel free to use the comment section below (you don't have to sing in, you can leave a comment as a guest).

Here is a Data Science Road Map for you

1. Educational Background

If you have a degree in Computer Science, Statistics, Math, Economics or other similar areas, you are in luck! Your educational background probably provided you a decent foundation to pursue a Data Science career. Feel free to skip this section.

If you have a degree in other area, you may have to learn a few additional things, but it's totally doable! I've seen people switching from all sorts of non-technical disciplines to web, software development and data science. How can you suplement an undergraduate or graduate degree?

Bootcamp

This option would be most comparable to getting a university degree in a data science related area. Quite a few Data Science bootcamps are even offered by universities. They can take up to 6 months to complete, and some have 9-5 schedule which may not work for you if you have a full-time job.

Bootcamps can also be quite costly although with some providers, you don't have to pay much up front and pay later once you successfully get a data scientist job.

A similar option would be a certificate degree offered by various universities. This option typically takes less time than a graduate program and costs less.

How do you find one? I'd recommend checking with your local universities about bootcamp or certificate programs. You can also search for organizations that offer bootcamps. Make sure to check their syllabus, reviews and even better if you talk to people who completed these programs (try finding them on LinkedIn) to see how much they learned and how easy it was for them to get a job after graduation.

In-person classes

Let's say you don't want to spend a fortune on a bootcamp or certificate program, but you prefer in-person experience. Your other option is to try searching for evening or weekend courses in your area. There are organizations that provide learning services which you may want to research.

Another option is to take undergraduate classes form a local university without pursuing a degree. I would be surprised if a data science program would allow you to take a class, but Statistics or Computer Science programs may.

I haven't taken any in-person Data Science courses, but I did take a Web Development and JavaScript courses from June college in Toronto, and I did enjoy learning something I wasn't familiar with in an in-person environment. I remember looking into General Assembly, but I ended up not pursuing it mainly because of the cost.

Online courses

What if there are no good programs near you or you want to minimize your spending? Your next option is online courses. This option will probably require more time on your end because not only would you need to study, you'll also have to find and organize your coursework. I also don't think it's sufficient to take one online course to pursue a data science carrer. Here is a list of courses that I would suggest looking into that should be enough to get you started.

  • Programming in Python (Coursera)
  • Programming in Python (Udacity)
  • Python for Data Science (Coursera)
  • Data Science Specialization (Coursera R)
  • Mathematics for Machine Learning (Coursera)
  • Machine Learning
  • Deep Learning (Coursera)

In addition to online courses, I would also suggest reading a couple of books.

  • Python Programming (Free, Beginner)
  • Fluent Python (Intermediate)
  • The Elements of Statistical Learning (R)
  • Applied Predictive Modeling (R)
  • Introduction to Machine Learning with Python
  • Hands-on Machine Learning with Scikit-Learn, Keras, and TensorFlow

2. Skills

As a data scientist, you will work on a variety of projects which will require a blend of technical and non-technical skills.

Data

You will need to get familiar with various data sources to be successful. Some of them will be internal and majority will be in a tabular format, example of which would be in relational databases, csv, flat filers. Other intenal data could be in non-tabular format and reside in NoSQL databases, such as document or graph.

You may also work with external data some of which may be publicly available whereas others would come from data vendors. Commonly the data can be supplied to you in tabular format, but for any real-time applications, the data will typically have to be accessed via an API which would require getting comfortable with non-tabular data.

You will need to be very comfortable with data manipulation including aggregation, different types of joins, filtering, variable creation, and so on. Ultimately, what helps me is knowing what my final data set should look like, so before you start working on a data project, make sure that you know what you are trying to achieve.

Another important non-technical aspect of data that's often overlooked is domain knowledge. You can construct a data set and even feed it to a fully automated machine learning model, but how do you know that the model uses relevant predictors? What if there is an attribute that represents data leakege? What if there is an attribute that's no longer relevant? How would you know which external data may be relevant? These questions can easily be answered if you have domain knowledge.

Data skills are absolutely necessary for predictive modeling. They are also important for reporting and ad-hoc purposes.

Data Visualization

This skill can come handy when you want to produce exploratory data analysis. It's also highly important if some of your projects are related to dashboard creation and reporting. Lastly, you'll most likely need to present your findings to your clients or management, and having compelling, easy-to-interpret data visualizations is necessary.

Predictive Modeling

I think many of us pick Data Science to build models. You may work on statistical, machine learning or deep learning models.

Besides knowing how to build a model in Python or R, you should know at least some basic math behind these models and various parameters that can impact the performance of your model. Here are some useful questions you should be able to answer. Do you need a highly accurate model or one that you can easily interpret? How are you going to ensure your model performs well on unseen data? Which model performance metrics you are most interested in? Are there any deployment limitations you may encounter?

Model Deployment

This may feel like a more of an IT task, but I'm a strong believer that it's something that needs to be done or at least closely supervised by a data scientist.

This skill requires more of a developer's mindset where you need to know best programming practices, potentially cloud architecture, etc.

Monitoring

Knowing how to monitor your projects results is also very important. You want to make sure you notice model drift if any as soon as possible. You would also want to monitor for any bugs or erros that end up being in production. Lastly, you'd want to monitor that the model you built performs equally well on new data.

Story telling

This is a soft skill that's commonly emphasized. Why is it important? You can have a great highly accurate model, but if you can't explain why your model is important and what business impact it can have, it may never make it into production.

Knowing how to translate technical stuff to a non-technical audience can be very challenging. I remember someone asking me in grad school, how I would explain what a linear regression is to a 9-year old and my first instinct was to talk about coefficients, least squares, homoskedasticity, linearity and mulicollinearity. I don't think that would've gone very well with a 9-year old. That's something you can think of! How would you answer this question?

3. Tools

One of my first posts was on various data science tools. You can check it out Here. I won't go into too many details, I'll just mention that the vast majority of data science problems can be solved with Python (possibly R), SQL and Cloud Services.

You'll most likely find yourself using SQL for data extraction and manipulation, so you should get comfortable with at least basic SQL commands. Here is a high level overview of those commands. I think SQL shouldn't be very challenging to learn for an absolute beginner.

The reason why I would pick Python over R as your choice of language is mainly because of model deployment. You can also check R/Python usage by industry and examples of problems that need to be solved (statistical analysis vs machine learning) and decide which language to go with.

Cloud Services wasn't really a requirement 7 years ago when I was applying for analytics jobs, however, these days, I expect people I interview have at least basic familiarity with cloud services.

You'll most likely end up hosting predictive models or analytics applications that you built on some server. It could be your company server, but chances are it'll be hosted on Amazon Web Service, Google Cloud or Microsoft Azure.

You would want to get familiar with varies ways of deploying analytics projects into production. It could be hosted on a virtual machine on a cloud, it could use containers or serverless architecture.

There are other concepts that you could look into, such as cloud storage, cloud databases, machine learning pipelines on the cloud, monitoring, etc.

4. Projects

There are tons of projects you can find on Kaggle and other places. I think it can be a great starting point especially if you use these projects to learn. The problem is that there are also tons of people working on these projects, so if you want to stand out, you really need to come up with something unique which may be challenging.

How do you pick a project? Is there something you are passionate about? If so, start searching for relevant data and see if there is possibly a machine learning or data exploration project.

No matter which project you pick, it should demonstrate that you are comfortable with every stage of a data science project. Is there an additional data source that you can join to your main data? Perhaps, there is an API and you can demonstrate that you are comfortable with making a request. You can also show that you know how to join data together. Are there charts or relationships that can demonstrate what you are trying to accomplish? Can you explain your thought process regarding feature engineering and model selection? When you are building a model, perhaps, you can start with something more explainable (regression or decision tree) and then proceed to a more sophisticated machine learning methodology. Could you add machine learning interpretability to explain predictions? Lastly, if you were to deploy this model, how would you approach it? Perhaps, you could build a flask app that a user could run locally and provide a sample request that they could experiment with. If you can ship it to the cloud or server, even better.

This may feel too much, but if you don't have a relevant educational or work background, you need to prove that you are capable of working on a real life data science project, and what I explained above is a good representation of one.

Final Thoughts

Being a data scientist is a lot of fun. I like how much new stuff comes out pretty much every year. It allows you to never stop learning, and if that's something you are in to, then you'll do just fine.

I hope this was somewhat useful and keep learning!
